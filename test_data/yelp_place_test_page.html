    I had booked a spot on the Noma waiting list about six months before my course in Copenhagen. I then waited along with a Danish friend to see if we would get a table.  We got the call and told that we had to be there in fifteen minutes. Not a problem.  

    You hear about places like this but I had never dined anywhere that had this reputation.

    The interior was simple and beautiful. It looked like a well laid out front room in a small Danish cottage that was expecting a number of people for dinner. Small but large. Quiet but loud. That contradiction set the tone for the entire evening.

    Noma sources it ingredients from the Danish landscape. Most them grown in the grounds around the restaurant. These tend to be small and intense in their flavours and they change with the seasons.

    The kitchen dominated the space. An area of precise swarming activity. There were roughly sixty chefs working on a variety of dishes. The culinary manifestations in production would soon be gracing our table. We had roughly 4-5 chefs producing each of the courses for us alone. They also took us for a tour after the meal. An absolutely spectacular place.

    In three hours we were provided 27 courses of taste and mind expanding food. I think it is similar to someone who has been colour blind and can suddenly see red or green. The world is different. The tastes you are exposed to are not alien or upsetting just new. Some people talk about taking photos of the dishes as they are presented. My friend and I decided that this was an evening for the tastebuds. Leave the images alone. I wanted to remember this time using my sense of taste.

    Noma sources it ingredients from the Danish landscape and produces amazing works of culinary art As I said there were a number of courses but I will only talk of a couple dishes created from locally sourced ingredients. The reindeer moss starter was an excellent way to begin. The amazing lemon ants  were spectacular. Finally the chocolate covered mushroom completely made me re-think my concept of sweet and savoury.

    It was not an easy meal to arrange and the price is something you would roughly expect for a in-kitchen tasters menu in a top London or New York restaurant.  

    If you have the chance - this is a once in a lifetime dining experience.

    It's rare that I find myself at a loss of words in a review, but that's where I am trying to figure out where to start this review of Noma.  Let's start with expectations...when you're eating at the "Best Restaurant in the World" they're high.  Let's just say Noma surpassed everything I could have expected in terms of taste, presentation, and service.

    The FOOD:  I could write up a review of all 21 courses we ate, but that would just be overwhelming.  Instead I'll highlight a few courses, 3 apps, 1 main, and a dessert and post the photos of them.

    --Fresh berries and lemon thyme:  The first dish which sounds rather simple was utterly divine.  Fresh berries in a slightly sweet and savory herbal sauce were incredibly refreshing.  What especially stood out was the grilled lemon thyme leaves sprinkled throughout the dish.  We knew we were off to a good start.

    --Beef tartar with ants:  I knew this dish was coming, and I expected it to be a gimmick.  The whole reason they serve it is that beef tartar needs some acid and there's no citrus that grows natively in Scandinavia...that's where the ants come in with their formic acid.  Completely in opposition to what the reasoning side of my brain was telling me, I absolutely loved the ants.  They were these little bursts of intense flavor.  We all decided that if people could get past the idea of eating ants that this would be the world's favorite snack food.

    --Burnt onion and walnuts:  A large spring onion is grilled at very high heat until the outside is charred.  Inside includes a stunning walnut oil drizzled over the onion...of course completely house made.

    --Squid and fennel:  First off, this is served in a stunning bowl made completely of ice.  Beautifully pure fresh, raw squid is served with fennel in a delightfully complex broth.  

    --Rhubarb crème fraiche and sorrel:  Doesn't that sound simple?  Nope!  The rhubarb is crafted in a process that takes 8-10 minutes per portion into a beautiful rose.  The flavors are both beautifully pure and perfectly meld.  By the way, the presentation throughout the entire meal was absolutely flawless.  

    I loved the crazy dishes from Æbleskiver that incorporated a grasshopper glaze to peas and radishes in which the peas tasted more "pea-like" than I could have ever imagined a pea tasting.  One or two dishes weren't exactly to my palate...the frozen cod liver did little for me and I didn't love the cooking of a potato in one dish; however, others found them delightful.  When you're pushing the edge, you might not love everything.

    The SERVICE:  Absolutely flawless. The perfect balance of attention with a casual delivery.  Everyone knew everything about the food and much of the food was delivered by the chefs that actually had just cooked it.  Our primary server was this awesome Austrian woman...we would have taken her with us if we had our own restaurant.  The pacing was relatively fast for the first 11 "tastes" as they put it and then much more slow paced for the main courses.  An Australian guy, who I presume was the sommelier knew his stuff.

    Speaking of the WINES...we went with the expensive, but oh-so-worth-it wine pairing. Every wine except for the last dessert rose pairing was white...most from lesser known producers in the Savoie, the Loire, and Austria.  Many of these fascinating wines had a spontaneous fermentation which led to an interesting complexity...sometimes with the slight sourness and brett flavor one finds in Belgian beers. The wine pairing really made the meal.  Pours were generous and refills happened for favorites.  The pregnant member of our group did the JUICE pairing.  We loved the spruce tips and apple juice, but I personally didn't find the juice pairing worth the cost.

    The ATMOSPHERE is incredible.  We were genuinely warmly welcomed by something like 15 of the staff as we walked in.  The beautiful dark wood table was warm and inviting with a view of the canal.  My seat had a lambskin on it...very luxe.  

    After dinner we were taken on a very cool tour of the kitchen...well 3 or 4 kitchens actually.  Something like 60 chefs were involved in preparing the food.  I really enjoyed meeting the Ph.D chemist who's heading their new fermentation lab.  The test kitchen looks fun...especially the white board with all the ideas they're throwing around for new dishes.  

    OVERALL, if you can score a reservation Noma is absolutely worth it.  Once you experience the Copenhagen prices for everything else (basically double North American prices), it's not even all that outrageous.  All four of us left incredibly satisfied, full, and ready to make our friends jealous with the photos of our crazy culinary experience.

    Listed in A Foodie's Paradise: Copenhagen, Denmark, Organic in CPH (Økologisk i KBH)

    We showed up to Noma almost a full hour before our reservation. We took a cab from the Christianshavn metro stop, which anyone who lives in Copenhagen knows defines us as either supremely lazy or far too exited to physically walk the 10 blocks. In reality I thought Noma was much farther down Strandgade than it actually was, so at 39kr when our cab driver announced that we had arrived, I was a touch embarrassed for myself, and for the fact that I only had one 20kr coin, which meant I had to charge the ride to my credit card.

    We stood outside absorbing the reality of our Right Now In Time for about 13 seconds before the maître d' with the perfectly groomed beard came out to greet us. What he was probably doing was making sure we weren't some rando tourists stalking René Redzepi, but he was super polite about his "Can we help you?" I was so excited I almost blurted out "WE HAVE A RESERVATION" in that sort of "I've Got The Golden Ticket!" way, and then contained myself, explaining that we were early and would be delighted if they could seat us in the bar while we waited for our table. Ah but nonsense!  Pishposh!  Those sorts of situations are easily resolved at Noma. They had a table for us straight away if we'd like to go ahead and start. Howlovelywedon'tmindifwedothankyou.

    I'm making Noma (and myself and N, my devout foodie friend) seem WAYYYY classier than any of us are in reality. What Noma is is an extremely cozy ("hyggelig"), down to earth, casual place to enjoy really expensive and delicious foodyoudidnotknowwasfood. And what N and I are isn't exactly classy so much as collective awesomeness (most recently defined as the following equation: awesome + awesome = EPIC) in a really good classy costume. We both work in banking. It comes with the title.

    ANYWAY, the food, because that's what we're all actually here for, isn't it?

    There were about 20 courses. For the sake of enjoyment and being a good story teller, I'm not going to break down every single detail. Instead, I'm going to tell you about the mind-blowing parts.

    Nordic Coconut
    So apparently they have coconuts in Scandinavia! Who says Denmark isn't tropical, huh? I still have no idea what these really are; if they really are called Nordic Coconuts and come as they are, picked off a Nordic palm tree filled with magic viking juice, or if they're some weird growth René found on the side of a tree somewhere, drained, and filled with a delightfully savory broth of freshly foraged herbs. The mystery may never be solved (but is most likely the latter).

    Æbleskiver and Greens
    Raise your hand if you're an æbleskiver fan?! Apparently Americans call these "round pancakes" (I had no idea?!) but they are EVERYWHERE during Christmas time in Copenhagen, and it's basically pancake batter fried in lard (if you're lucky, vegetable oil if you're not), covered in powdered sugar, and dipped in a delicious runny sugary jam. The above is NOTHING like that mostly because it had vegetables on the inside, but it was equally FUCKING AWESOME.

    Pickled and Smoked Quail Egg
    I also forgot to ask how this one was done, but what happens is, you lift the lid off of that precious little box in the shape of an egg, a plume of smoke comes out, and these adorable little quail babies are sitting there perched on hay. I suspect René was inspired by Portlandia's "We Can Pickle That" segment for most of his menu, by the way; half our menu was fermented. Anyway, these eggs were soft-boiled, then smoked, then fermented. They were served warm and the yolks were perfectly cooked, so I kind of get a mind fuck (pardon my french) trying to figure out the mechanics of it all.

    Onion and fermented pears (with ants)
    This had ants in it. I ate real ants and I liked it. A lot. That is all.

    Cauliflower and pine, cream and horseradish
    Speaking of Christmas, this dish tasted like it. They do not advise that you eat the pine branches laying on top of the cauliflower (something about them not digesting well, apparently it's been tried), and by the way, that was the best triangle of cauliflower that I have eaten in my entire life. I know, I read what I just wrote and I understand how ridiculous it sounds, but I'm serious you guys.

    Chocolate covered pork skin with blueberries
    Savory and sweet is totally my jam and I'm a huge fan of everything involving pork and chocolate, so when this delightful treat - the fourth of the dessert courses and the final course of the evening - came out to be paired with my coffee, I actually had a foodgasm. It was embarrassing and slightly awkward. I blame the bottomless wine pairing for my lack of self control.

    To sum up: Best Meal Of My Life. Worth The Wait. Wish I Could Go Back Tomorrow.

    Listed in Michelin Guide ala KQN, Denmark, The World's 50 Best

    Copenhagen has become one of my favorite places in the world, especially to eat. I've been through Denmark a number of times now, and I still love the place as much as ever. After watching the "Copenagen" episode of Parts Unknown, the Zero Point Zero team & Tony Bourdain certainly did it a lot of justice. It's just too bad that they didn't have the time to capture more of the culinary offerings, the art, architecture, landscape, and of course the wonderful people of this area. Im fortunate enough to have made many friends and acquaintances there now, and always look forward to going back. That episode made me nostalgic for things and I'd just been there a few month ago!

    So to that, this update is overdue.

    Since I last dined at Noma, they have remodeled the dining area. The wood tones are darker, things look a bit sleeker and not as rustic.

    They were in the process of building the new test kitchen last time which is now complete and chef Lars Williams has moved off the Nordic Food Lab boat to run said kitchen. Ben Reade has taken his place.

    The NFL boat has been moved much closer to the restaurant itself.

    There's a ton of construction which last time was in the form of a diorama the last time I saw it. Walking from CIty Hall is a decent trek and was compounded by that construction. A foot bridge from Nyhavn is being built and I believe the next time Im here it will be finished. My walks from City Hall will be easier but then every one and their tourist mom will come over too. The barbarians are at the gate!

    Chef Dan Giusti who gave me first ever tour here is now the head honcho at the pass. Previous honcho Matt Orlando has opened his own place Amass with previous Noma alumn Bo Bratlan (& Claus Meyer's Restaurant Radio right before). Two sous, Samuel Nutter and Victor Wagman have also opened their own place, Restaurant Bror. Add these to Relae from Kim Rossen & Christian Puglisi and you have a plethora of ex Noma doing amazing CPH cuisine.

    Shortly after I left apparently Bourdain, Porcelli, and the ZPZ team showed up to film the episode (they got the Summer menu after our Spring one). Shorty after that, Noma shut down to demolish and rebuild their main kitchen. For those that saw the Parts Unknown episode, I suspect many things will already be different. One thing I can say however, a restaurant like Noma has to evolve. However one thing that doesnt change is that it is creative, it is delicious, and it provides excellent hospitality. ...

    So this last trip was a weird one. I had made a reservation and it turned out my friend had made one the day before? So I cancel mine and join my friends who come up from Munich by train. I take the twenty minute walk from Rådhuspladsen as it's a strangely nice week in CPH. Normally it's 10 degrees C and this week it's 20. As I had mentioned, there's a ton of construction going on which makes walking through Christianhavn a bit of a chore. Plus it's unusually hot. But I finally make it, and I see a few familiar faces from the front of the house. I admire the remodel and have a drink in the lounge as I wait for my friends. They show and I find that one of them has had their wallet/passport/credit cards/etc stolen on the train. Not a great way for them to start the trip. However because we were at Noma, things would soon subside and we would be under the restaurants spell.

    The series of snacks come in a whirlwind. I decide what the heck, Ill do both the wine and juice pairing. Double fisting beverages is something Im good at. Let the marathon begin:

    -Malt Flatbreads
    -Hollowed out potato with consomme (like a coconut!)
    -Fried Reindeer Moss with Cep Powder
    -"Berries and Roses"
    -Coriander cookies
    -Some sort of leaves and paste (look at the pic I dont quite remember)
    -Dehydrated carrots and ash

The moss and the charred leeks were also a pleasant surprise!

    After the nibbles, the dining slowed down and we were fed at a more normal pace with wine pairings for each of the courses. They do some really interesting things at Noma, and because of that I can see why they are ranked as one of the top restaurants in the world. The chef comes up with interesting ways to flavor things including using ants in the beef tartare because there is no citrus fruit that grows in Nordic countries, so the ants give the beef the acidity needed for the tartare. Neat! They even served scallops that have been dehydrated into little crisps instead of serving in the normal way. They also did a savory berry dish that was unexpected. I mean, the interesting preparation of food is a change from how I've eaten these foods normally, but ultimately I think I prefer my tartare with lemon (as the ants were TOO acidic), my scallops cooked normally and my berries in desserts. The best for me was potato and plum dessert that tasted almost of marzipan. We also had a sweet Rosé wine with the desserts that was delicious, and hopefully we'll be able to find it to have at home. As for the rest of the wines, they picked a nice selection to go with the courses.

    We then retired to the lounge to finish with some delicious coffee (again the Danish know how to make REALLY good coffee), aquavit and some sweet nibbles (that were much sweeter than the desserts). And for me the best was the danish pastry. Flaky, buttery and sweet: the best Danish I've eaten. We were then taken for a tour of the kitchen, the private dining room and the upstairs kitchens. While the food was tasty and well prepared, for me the unusual style kind of took away from the flavor, and the dishes that were simple and straightforward were a bit bland and boring. I still can't fault it, because that's the style they are going for, and the whole experience was definitely worth the trip.

    This is the most unique and creative restaurant I've ever tried.  Noma's innovation spans many techniques and is a guided tour through nature, exploring plants and animals that I didn't even know existed.

    You will not be getting a lot of red meat or red wines in the standard wine pairing. The kitchen has a strong preference for playing with vegetables matched with unique wines (mostly white/orange - yes, there is such a thing as orange wine!) and they do a fantastic job at it.  But it may disappoint people with different expectations.

    Service was top notch. Very relaxed, yet effective.  There was not a single flaw the entire night, and service was completely unpretentious, contrary to many fine dining places.

    Out of the 20 servings (11 bite-sized appetizers, 9 courses), I liked everything and have too many favorites to list.  Because the food is so unique, some of the wines are, too.  They are all great matches, but I wasn't the big fan of all of the sweeter wines.

    But when you know what to expect, this restaurant definitely proves why it has been considered one of the two best restaurants in the world for some years now.  They are not just serving amazing food, but have defined a whole new restaurant category with impact far beyond the beautiful rooms on Strandgade  in Copenhagen.

    You have a choice of wine or juice pairing.  I highly recommend the juices and you can always order wines by the glass, if you do feel it's necessary with some wines to round out the experience.

    Listed in Trancending the Profession

    Okay, okay, there's more than enough press/blogs/twitter accounts/etc out there that you can find all the info you could ever ask for about noma...There are two cookbooks, the nordic food lab (nordicfoodlab.com) makes regular updates, Chef Rene's pretty regularly in magazines...keeps his own twitter account and generally has no problem sharing the inner workings of this restaurant.

    Instead of trying to cover everything about noma, I'll just tell one little story:
    There's one dish on the menu that I became very familiar with over time.  It's a single quail egg.  

    To get to your table, that egg's journey begins in the morning, when it is carefully soft boiled by one cook upstairs.  (And no thermal circulator either...just good old fashioned pot of water on the stove and a cook).  The egg is then peeled by hand...Any mistakes are rejected.  crack the white?  Rejected.  Nick it just a tiny bit?  Rejected.  Too firm?  Rejected.  Shape off?  Rejected.  noma typically has 90 people a day sit down to eat, so that's a lot of peeling going on.  

    About 15 minutes before service begins, the peeled eggs are carefully placed in a smoking pan and smoked for mere minutes.  Go too far and you can lose a whole pan of eggs...Before every service, the staff gather in the dining room to have the pre-service meeting.  There, they go over the guests for that service, the menu and other random details.  

    As soon as the meeting finishes, one person must RUN up the stairs with the seating details.  That person then takes the freshly smoked quail eggs and carefully places them into small bags that are also labeled on the spot...Two eggs for a table of two, 4 for a four and so on.  You want to have the eggs match in size for every table.  They're super fragile so breakage is a big issue.  On the other had the first guests are about to arrive, so you're definitely under the gun to get this all done. The bags are then dosed with a pickling brine and then sealed.  They're carefully placed into a pan, again to avoid breakage, and they're run down stairs to the snack station where they're held in a warm bath.  

    Just before they're served, they're placed on a bed of hay, salted, then smoked one more time before they're walked out to your table.  Sure, there are possibly easier ways to do this, but everything about this process exists to ensure the integrity of the dish.  Everything happens just at the last minute.

    So like I said, I'd become familiar with this dish...since I was the schmo who did all of the running up and down the stairs.  I'd eat rejects regularly just to check.  But then the day came when I was able to sit down in the dining room.  That day, I didn't have to do the running.  I just sat there and this quail egg came to the table.  I opened up the dish, grabbed the egg and popped it into my mouth.  It quite literally brought me to tears.  It was like nothing I had experienced before...which was the weirdest feeling.  I helped make this thing every day.  Tasted it every day.  But it wasn't until I had it as a diner that I got to see the dish fully realized.  The temperature, the smoke, the salt, the pickle, all the work...when it all came together it was a completely different experience.  

    And that's the thing about the dishes...they are a lot of work, they take a tremendous amount of skill and care to get to your table, sometimes the work doesn't even seem to make sense...but for the person sitting at the table, all you ever see is something that is at its absolute peak, and it will only be at that peak for a few minutes at most.  It's something special, unique and fleeting...a glimpse into something that is otherwise unknowable.  And that is the gift that the cooks give to each guest.

    Not bad for a bunch of seal fuckers!
